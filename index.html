The Physics Behind Advanced Generative AI

Posted on: 2023-01-15

Last week, OpenAI released Sora. It’s still evolving, but it inspired me to share how models like DALL·E, Midjourney, and Stable Diffusion came to existence. In 2015, researchers from Stanford and Berkeley introduced diffusion models in their paper "Deep Unsupervised Learning using Nonequilibrium Thermodynamics." The idea was inspired by the same natural laws that govern how milk diffuses in your morning coffee. Full Article: https://lnkd.in/gQ9x9kZY The concept is simple: 1. Start with an image. 2. Gradually add random noise in each step until it’s unrecognizable—like TV static. 3. Train a neural network that learns how to reverse this process, step by step. Once trained, the model generates new, realistic images from pure noise—like rewinding the diffusion of ink in water. More info: https://lnkd.in/gZAYrgMz This isn’t the only time physics has fueled AI. Earlier work by AI pioneers John Hopfield and Geoffrey Hinton, which earned them the 2024 Nobel Prize in "Physics", highlights how ideas flow both ways. AI has also provided powerful tools for physical scientists to push the boundaries of their fields. In fact, the cover image of this post was created using a diffusion-based model with the following prompt: “A droplet of ink diffused in water, the tail of the diffused ink has shaped a brain.”
